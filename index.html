Borneo bears the burden

Sabah and Sarawak Malaysia's two Bornean states account for the majority of total forest loss, with Sarawak alone losing over 3.4 million hectares between 2001 and 2024.
These states are home to some of the world's oldest and most biodiverse rainforests, yet they face relentless pressure from logging, palm oil expansion, and infrastructure development.

The Wildlife Paying the Price

Habitat loss directly threatens Malaysia's extraordinary biodiversity. The IUCN Red List tracks species by their risk of extinction, and Malaysia's numbers are alarming.

A biodiversity crisis hiding in plain sight

Malaysia is home to 2,379 threatened species as of 2024 — one of the highest counts in Southeast Asia.
Plants make up the vast majority at 1,543 species, yet they receive far less public attention than charismatic animals like the Malayan tiger or Bornean orangutan.

Every threatened plant species represents a potential collapse in the food web that thousands of animals depend on.

Are We Doing Enough?

Malaysia has recently started to make changes with the introduction of the Ecological Fiscal Transfer (EFT), which channels government funds to states for biodiversity protection. But how much is actually being spent, and is it working?

Annual EFT allocation in RM million. The dashed line shows the RM 1 billion annual allocation target called for by conservationists

Of RM 517 million disbursed between 2019–2024, only RM 25 million across four states had documented spending outcomes.

Where did the money go?

Of RM 517 million disbursed between 2019 and 2024, only RM 25 million across four states Johor, Sabah, Pahang, and Kelantan had any documented spending outcomes.
The remaining RM 492 million< has no public record of how it was spent, raising serious questions about conservation accountability in Malaysia.
