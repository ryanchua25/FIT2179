Section 1 - The Threat

Forests are the foundation of Malaysia's biodiversity.
But decades of logging, agriculture, and development have taken a severe toll.
Here is a look at how much has been lost and what is driving it.

Annual tree cover loss has remained consistently high since 2001. The vertical marker highlights 2019, when Malaysia introduced the Ecological Fiscal Transfer.

Forest loss remains persistent

Malaysia lost millions of hectares of tree cover between 2001 and 2024. Even after conservation funding was introduced in 2019, annual losses remained substantial.

This shows that funding alone is not enough unless it is matched with stronger protection, clearer spending records and effective state-level action.

Section 2 - Where

Forest loss is not evenly distributed across Malaysia. Some states show heavier pressure than others, revealing that forest decline is not only concentrated in Borneo but also affects smaller Peninsular states.

Darker states show where forest loss affects a larger share of state land.
Hover over each state to compare forest pressure across Malaysia.

Smaller states show serious forest pressure

Sabah and Sarawak still record the largest total forest losses because they cover much larger land areas. However, the map shows that forest pressure is also intense in smaller Peninsular states.

Malacca, Johor and Negeri Sembilan stand out because a large share of their land area has been affected by tree cover loss. This shows that Malaysia’s forest issue is not only concentrated in Borneo.

The ranking highlights which states show the highest forest pressure relative to their land area.

Section 3 - What Is at Stake

Habitat loss directly threatens Malaysia's extraordinary biodiversity. The IUCN Red List tracks species by their risk of extinction, and Malaysia's numbers are alarming.

A biodiversity crisis hiding in plain sight

Malaysia is home to 2,379 threatened species as of 2024, one of the highest counts in Southeast Asia.
Plants make up the vast majority at 1,543 species, yet they receive far less public attention than charismatic animals like the Malayan tiger or Bornean orangutan.

Every threatened plant species represents a potential collapse in the food web that thousands of animals depend on.

Number of Critically Endangered, Endangered, and Vulnerable species in Malaysia by group.

Plants form the largest share of threatened species. This shows that biodiversity loss is not only about animals, but entire ecosystems.

Section 4 - The Response

Malaysia has recently started to make changes with the introduction of the Ecological Fiscal Transfer (EFT), which channels government funds to states for biodiversity protection. But how much is actually being spent, and is it working?

Annual EFT allocation in RM million.
The dashed line shows the RM 1 billion annual allocation target called for by conservationists

Where did the money go?

Of RM 517 million disbursed between 2019 and 2024, only RM 25 million across four states Johor, Sabah, Pahang, and Kelantan had any documented spending outcomes.
The remaining RM 492 million has no public record of how it was spent, raising serious questions about conservation accountability in Malaysia.

Protected Land Area in Malaysia Over Time

Percentage of Malaysia's total land area under formal protection

Protection did not rise after EFT began. Although the Ecological Fiscal Transfer was introduced in 2019 to support conservation, Malaysia’s protected land share appears to decline after its introduction. This suggests that funding alone does not guarantee stronger protection unless it is matched with clear action, monitoring and long-term land protection.
